Flowers for Algernon - Daniel Keyes


1 Sentence Summary: Charlie Gordon, a mentally disabled man, becomes a genius with an operation, a genius who doesn't know who he is.

Favorite Quote: "Intelligence and education that hasn't been tempered by human affection isn't worth a damn."

About the Author: Daniel Keyes was born in 1927 in New York City. Keyes received a bachelor's in psychology from Brooklyn College. Keyes became a writer and editor at several comic magazines. He worked under Stan Lee. Keyes wrote a story called "Brainstorm" but didn't submit it to Lee. Later, that story turned into "Flowers for Algernon" in 1966. Keyes got inspired for the story "Brainstorm" while teaching at a high school. His works are usually sci-fi. It's no surprise since he worked at comic magazines.

About the Time Book Was Written: The novel version of the book was published in 1966. The book has many notions about the science and its workings. Keye's likely got inspired by the rise of scientific advancements around that time. Psychoanalysis ideas of Carl Jung and Sigmund Freud were also prominent during that time. We see related notions such as subconsciousness, family relationships, etc. It becomes evident when the main character finds similarities between his lover and mother.

Summary and Thoughts: It's a book where you can relate to the main character, Charlie Gordon, in one way or another. His psyche changes constantly throughout the book, yet it's still him. We dive into psychology as a science. The author explains research principles, concepts, and what makes research valuable. We dive into psychology as a philosophy as well. We observe the times that Charlie sees his other self in his subconsciousness.

We also get to see the author's view on people and intelligence. As Charlie gets more intelligent, he walks towards a purpose. He wants to discover and create. His love, friends and even himself come after that. As his intelligence deteriorates, he watches a woman naked through his apartment window and watches TV shows even though he knows this poisons his mind.


3 Lessons

We're the Results of Our Surroundings.

As Charlie discovers himself rather rapidly, he finds the roots of all his distresses in his home. During his childhood, with his family and surroundings. They shaped his character.

Lesson 2: You Can't Control Everything.

Charlie is the epitome of intelligence. However, it doesn't matter how much he's capable; he can't stop his inevitable intelligence decrease.

Lesson 3: We Need Other People.

Even flowers, cats, or other animals can't do without the presence of another living creature. Without love. It applies to humans times more. Even if it's a mouse, we need someone that we think understands us, whom we do understand, just like Charlie and Algernon.
